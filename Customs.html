Hanbok Style

Hanbok represents the elegance and history of Korean fashion. Learn about its symbolism and regional variations. It is rarely worn in daily life today but is still used for special occasions and cultural events such as weddings, tarditional ceremony, baby first birthday and festivals. If you are interested to try wearing hanbok, when going to Korea, you can actually try to rent hanboks at their famous palaces such as Gyeongbokgung Palace, Changdeokgung Palace and more! This will add more fun experince to the trip!

History

Korean traditions trace back centuries, shaped by Confucian values, dynastic culture, and regional identity.

Symbolism

Traditional customs hold symbolic meaning: colors represent virtues, gestures show respect, and rituals reflect harmony.

Bowing Etiquette

New Year Bow (Man)

New Year Bow (Woman)

These bows are performed during Seollal (Korean New Year), expressing gratitude, respect, and well-wishes for the year ahead. Kids who showed gratitude towars the elderly can also receive money as a gift called "Sebae Don" from the elders.

Funeral Bow (Man)

Funeral Bow (Woman)

Funeral bows are deeper, slower, and more solemn. They reflect mourning, sincerity, and deep respect for the departed and their family. Not just family members, but guests that came will also perform this bow to show their respect to the dead.

Ancestral Rites

Ancestral rites, known as Jesa, honor ancestors through ritual offerings, food preparation, and bowing traditions. These ceremonies 9 are often done on the day of the ancestor's death anniversary or during major holidays like Chuseok and Seollal. Jesa reflects filial piety, respect for heritage, and the importance of family bonds in Korean culture.
